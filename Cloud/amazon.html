Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) - Amazon Web Services

Infrastructure as a service (IaaS) is a form of cloud computing that provides virtualized computing resources over the internet. In an IaaS model, a cloud provider hosts the infrastructure components traditionally present in an on-premises data center, including servers, storage and networking hardware. The IaaS provider also supplies a range of services to accompany those infrastructure components. These can include detailed billing, monitoring, log access, security, load balancing and clustering, as well as storage resiliency, such as backup, replication and recovery.

Advantages

  • Easier, faster and more cost-efficient to operate a workload without having to buy, manage and support the underlying infrastructure.
  • IaaS is an effective model for workloads that are temporary, experimental or that change unexpectedly.
  • In general, IaaS customers pay on a per use basis, typically by the hour, week or month, eliminating large capital expenses.

Disadvantages

  • Despite its flexible, pay-as-you-go model, IaaS billing can be a problem for some businesses
  • Because IaaS providers own the infrastructure, the details of their infrastructure configuration and performance are rarely transparent to IaaS users.
  • If an IaaS provider experiences network bottlenecks or any form of internal or external downtime, the users' workloads will be affected.

Amazon Web Services (AWS) is a subsidiary of Amazon.com that provides on-demand cloud computing platforms to individuals, companies and governments, on a paid subscription basis.

The AWS platform was launched in July 2002, in the beginning, the platform consisted of only a few disparate tools and services.

In April 2015, Amazon.com reported AWS was profitable, with sales of $1.57 billion in the first quarter of the year and $265 million of operating income.
